Field Notes: Rewilding the Kestrel Valley Watershed

Posted by Elena Marsh, Conservation Correspondent — March 4th

Three years ago, the Kestrel Valley watershed was a straightened, concrete-lined channel that flooded twice a year and supported almost no native fish. Today, after a community-led restoration project, otters have returned for the first time since the 1980s.

How the project began

The initiative started with a single landowner, Tomas Reyes, who offered eleven acres of riverside pasture to a local trust in exchange for a long-term conservation easement. Within eight months, the trust had secured funding to remove 400 meters of concrete channel lining.

Reintroducing native vegetation

Volunteers planted over 3,000 willow and alder saplings along the newly meandering banks. The root systems stabilize the soil far better than the original grass cover did, cutting erosion rates by an estimated 60 percent according to early monitoring data.

Measurable results so far

Water quality samples taken this winter show dissolved oxygen levels up 22 percent compared to the 2021 baseline. Brown trout, absent from formal surveys for over a decade, were recorded spawning in the upper reaches last November.

What comes next

The trust is now applying for a second grant to extend the restoration another two kilometers downstream, with construction tentatively scheduled to begin next spring, pending a final ecological impact assessment.
